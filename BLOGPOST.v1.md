# SAE interpretability conventions don't transfer cleanly to world models

Sparse autoencoders have become the default lens for mechanistic interpretability on language models, and a small ecosystem of tooling has grown up around them — Neuronpedia, feature dashboards, activation-ranked discovery panels, intervention sliders. The tooling encodes a set of conventions: rank features by activation magnitude, give each feature a stable page, let the user steer the model by scaling a feature's direction. Those conventions are now being carried into other domains, world models among them, on the assumption that an SAE is an SAE and the surrounding machinery comes along for free.

It mostly doesn't. This post is about the specific ways the LLM-SAE conventions failed when I imported them into a world model, found by building the tooling and then characterizing what went wrong. The substrate is IRIS, a transformer world model trained on Atari Breakout; the tool is an interactive feature-explorer of the kind that exists for LLMs but not for world models. But the tool is the substrate, not the artifact. The artifact is a catalog of conventions that need adapting before they're trustworthy on a world model, each one found the hard way.

There are six findings. They are individually subtle and collectively substantial, and they reinforce each other: every convention I tested transferred worse than I expected, and a couple transferred in ways that quietly produced wrong answers while looking like they worked. The central one — that ranking features by activation magnitude surfaces the wrong features on a world model, with a specific worked example of a user being shown a feature whose meaning doesn't match what they were looking at — is the one I'd most want an interpretability researcher to take away. The others are the supporting case.

The shape worth flagging up front, because it's the spine of the post: the most interesting finding came out of a sequence of "found a confound, fixed it, found a deeper problem, decided what to ship." I built a causal-importance ranking, discovered it had collapsed into a magnitude ranking in disguise, fixed the confound, discovered the fix had exposed a deeper single-state fragility, measured how fragile, and then decided — against a threshold I'd committed to in advance — not to ship it as a discovery ranking at all. Most interpretability writeups present the final method as if it arrived clean. This one shows the wrestling, because the wrestling is where the transferable lessons are.

## Context: the project and the prior work

What I built is an interactive tool for inspecting and steering SAE features in the residual stream of an IRIS-class world model. IRIS ("Transformers are Sample-Efficient World Models", Micheli et al.) is a transformer trained to imagine Atari rollouts: it tokenizes each game frame into a small set of discrete tokens with a VQ-VAE, then autoregressively predicts the next frame's tokens given past frames and an action. The agent and the world model are separate networks; the world model is the part that "dreams" the game forward, and it's the part with the residual stream I trained SAEs on.

The tool does what an LLM SAE explorer does, transposed to this substrate. It runs the model live, extracts residual-stream activations through forward hooks, runs them through a trained SAE, and presents the features on a canvas where you can pin them, label them, search for them, and — the part that matters for a world model — intervene on them: push a feature's direction into the residual stream and watch the model's imagined rollout diverge from baseline over the next several frames. The point of the tool was never the tool. The personal goal was skill-building toward AI-safety research engineering, and the research goal was to characterize what's different about doing SAE interpretability on a world model versus an LLM. The findings below are that characterization.

The methodological discipline that surfaced these findings carried over from a prior project on factored SAEs for LLM concept manifolds ([{{FACTORED_SAE_LINK}}]). I won't recap it. The one thing worth naming, because it's load-bearing in several findings here, is a habit that project beat into me: treat any result from a single measurement as provisional, in both directions — a single-seed negative is as untrustworthy as a single-seed positive. Three separate times across the two projects, a single-measurement result misled a writeup: once a protocol bug, once a noisy negative reported as clean, once a noisy positive amplified by sampling luck. The corollary is matched-dimension evaluation skepticism — when you change a measurement's setup to fix one problem, re-check that the fix didn't quietly recreate the thing you were measuring. Both habits caught real errors in this project, and I'll point them out where they did.

A note on the SAE itself, since it grounds everything downstream. It's a plain ReLU sparse autoencoder with an L1 penalty (`backend/sae.py`), trained on layer-5 residual-stream activations harvested from Breakout rollouts — 256-dimensional input, 2048 features (8× expansion), L1 coefficient 2.0. About 80k activation vectors went into it, from roughly 4,700 frames at 17 token positions each. The trained SAE has an L0 of around 14–15 (the interpretable band is usually quoted as 10–50) and a sharp magnitude falloff in its top features, so it behaves like a normal, healthy SAE. That's worth establishing because most of the findings below are *not* "the SAE is bad." The SAE is fine. The conventions around it are what break.

## Finding 1: tokenizer compression limits pixel-space state extraction

The first thing I wanted from the tool was behavioral: intervene on a feature, then read out what changed in the game by extracting object positions — paddle x, ball x and y — from the imagined frames and plotting their trajectories. This is the obvious move. If a feature is "the ball," scaling it should move the ball, and you'd see that in the extracted ball position.

It doesn't work, and the reason is structural rather than incidental. IRIS tokenizes each observation into 16 discrete tokens. The decoded reconstruction from those 16 tokens keeps the coarse spatial structure — you can see roughly where the paddle is, the brick layout is legible — but small or fast-moving objects don't survive the round trip. The Breakout ball is the canonical casualty. It's a few pixels; after the VQ-VAE compresses the frame to 16 tokens and decodes it back, the ball is below the effective resolution. A brightness-blob extractor run over the imagined frames found zero balls across every frame it looked at; paddle x came back stuck at 0.5 (the extractor's default when it finds nothing usable); and the maximum brightness in the region where the ball should be topped out around 0.56, where a present ball would saturate. The information isn't degraded, it's gone.

It gets worse, in a way I didn't expect and that's worth isolating as its own point. I assumed the loss was specific to the 16-token *reconstruction* — that the model's input was fine-grained and only the imagined output was lossy. It isn't. The ball is sub-pixel in the 64×64 observation the model actually consumes, before any tokenization. When I needed ground-truth collision labels later (Finding 4), I could not get them from the model's own input frame; I had to label from the original 210×160 Atari frame, where ball and paddle are still resolvable, and align those labels back to the model's timeline. The trap here is assuming the model's input resolution is fine enough to extract labels from. On this substrate it isn't, and the failure is silent — the extractor returns a number, the number is just always the same wrong number.

The workaround was to stop trying to read pixels and measure the thing the substrate can actually give you: token divergence. For each imagined step I count how many of the 16 observation tokens differ between the baseline rollout and the intervened rollout. That signal is always present, it directly measures "the imagined dynamics diverged," and it doesn't depend on any object being resolvable in pixel space. Per-step mean pixel difference rides alongside it as a secondary, coarser signal. Pixel-space object extraction stays in the code for the large persistent objects where it works, but it's flagged honestly wherever it fails rather than silently returning 0.5.

The transferable version: pixel-space behavioral interpretability on tokenized world models is fundamentally limited by the tokenizer's spatial resolution, and the limit can reach all the way up into the model's input, not just its reconstructions. Anyone trying to do "intervene and read the behavioral change off the pixels" on an IRIS-class model will hit this. Token-level divergence is the more honest observable. Per-object pixel extraction is fine for large objects and fails silently for small ones, so if you keep it, annotate the failures — a number that's always 0.5 is not a measurement.

## Finding 2: single-frame priming produces near-static rollouts

Once token divergence was the observable, I could run the actual experiment: pause at a game state, imagine 20 frames forward under baseline, imagine 20 frames forward under intervention with the identical action sequence, and compare. The intervention signal was real — token divergence scaled with intervention magnitude, running from about 2.1 tokens at low magnitude to about 4.5 at high. But characterizing the *baseline* turned up something that limits how strongly any of these dynamics claims can be made.

The baseline rollout barely moves. Across 20 imagined frames, the mean pixel change is about 0.8 out of 255. The imagined scene is, to a first approximation, still.

The reason is that the rollout is primed from a single observation frame. One frame tells the world model where everything is but not how anything is moving — it has position but not velocity. With no temporal context to establish that the ball is travelling down-and-left at some speed, the model doesn't have a confident dynamics to roll forward, so it mostly holds the scene where it is. This is a property of single-frame priming, not a bug; you'd fix it by feeding the model several recent frames to establish velocity, but that's a change to how you drive the world model, not a tuning knob.

The consequence for the intervention experiment is specific and I want to be precise about it rather than wave it away. The divergence-from-baseline signal is genuine and it scales with magnitude, so "this intervention changes the imagined rollout" is supported. But the baseline it's measured against is a near-static scene, not typical dynamic play. So what the experiment cleanly demonstrates is closer to "the feature changes the static reconstruction the model settles into" than "the feature changes how the game evolves over time." The stronger dynamics claim — feature X alters the trajectory of a moving game — is only as strong as the baseline is dynamic, and this baseline isn't very.

The transferable version: any world-model rollout experiment has to account for what its baseline actually represents. A near-static baseline isn't wrong, but it quietly narrows what a divergence-from-baseline result can claim, and it's easy to report the divergence without reporting that the thing it diverged from was barely moving. Measure your baseline's own dynamics before you interpret divergence from it.

## Finding 3: argmax decoding limits intervention smoothness

A thing you expect from a clean SAE, coming from the LLM side, is that intervention response is reasonably smooth: nudge a feature a little, get a little effect; nudge it more, get more. On this substrate it isn't smooth, and the reason isn't the SAE.

IRIS decodes each next-observation token by taking an argmax over the token logits, and the logits are confident — the distribution over the 512-entry codebook is peaked. Argmax over a peaked distribution is a hard step function. The decoded token doesn't change at all until the intervention is large enough to flip which codebook entry wins, and then it changes discontinuously. In practice this means interventions need to be large to do anything visible — roughly magnitude 50 before there's any effect, roughly 100 for a clear one — and the effect, once it appears, is small and non-monotonic rather than a smooth ramp.

The thing to notice is where the bottleneck is. It's tempting to read "interventions are jagged and need huge magnitudes" as a sign the SAE could be better. But a higher-quality SAE doesn't soften a step function at the decoder. The decoder argmaxes regardless of how clean the feature direction feeding into it is. The non-smoothness is downstream of the dictionary entirely.

This relocates the fix. Getting smoother intervention response isn't a matter of training a better SAE; it's a method change at the decoding step — injecting the intervention across all token positions instead of one, scaling the injection per feature magnitude, or changing the decode from argmax to something softer. Those are method-development projects, not tuning passes.

The transferable version: smoothness of intervention response is a property of the model's architecture, specifically its decoding step, not a property of the feature dictionary. On an LLM you can often treat the SAE as the steering surface and expect graded response. On a world model with a hard discrete decoder, the decoder sets the granularity, and no amount of SAE quality buys past it. If your intervention response is jagged, look at the decoder before you blame the dictionary.

## Finding 4: magnitude ranking doesn't surface event-correlated features

This is the central finding and the best-evidenced, so it gets the most room.

The default discovery surface in an LLM SAE tool is a list of features ranked by activation magnitude on the current input. It's a good default for LLMs: each token is a discrete decision point, the features that fire hardest are the ones contributing most to that decision, and a user scanning the top of the list is looking at the features that matter for the token in front of them. The convention is so standard it's invisible. My v1 tool had it: a panel showing the top-firing features for the current frame.

The first sign it was wrong was a UX symptom. The list churned. Because frames in a world model are highly correlated and the underlying state changes slowly, "top-firing right now" is dominated by small movements in the tails of a slow, high-dimensional state, and the ranking reshuffles frame to frame. By the time you'd read the list and decided which feature to pin, it had changed under you. That alone told me magnitude was a poor *navigational* default here — it ranks by a noisy statistic on correlated inputs. But the churn was the surface of a deeper problem, and finding the deeper problem took a diagnostic battery.

The battery came out of a concrete complaint I had while using the tool: the features looked *decoupled* from on-screen events. I'd watch the top-ranked feature during a paddle-ball collision — exactly the moment I expected a "collision feature" to fire — and it would sit flat, while firing happily when the ball was nowhere near the paddle. Either the features were broken, mislabeled, polysemantic, or being mis-measured. I ran five tests to find out which. Two of them mattered enough to walk through; one of them caught a methodological error I'd otherwise have shipped.

**First, I ruled out the mechanical explanations.** Test 1 checked whether the frame and the activations shown beside it were even from the same model step. They almost weren't: the displayed frame was being captured *after* the environment stepped, while the activations described the *pre-step* observation, so the frame led its own activations by exactly one step. I measured this as a clean +1 lag — over candidate shifts, the one minimizing mean absolute difference between the displayed frame and the activation observation (both downsampled to 64×64 gray) was +1 for the old capture and 0 after the fix — reproducible across two episodes. It's worth dwelling on because it's a trap anyone building a live viewer can fall into: I'd bundled the frame and activations into a single message, which guarantees they *travel and render together*, and it's natural to assume that also means they describe the same step. It doesn't. The offset lived inside the single message. A one-frame offset is small, but it is *precisely* the artifact a human reads as "the feature fires just before the event, not during it." Fixed by capturing the frame before stepping. Test 2 confirmed the extraction itself is bit-exact deterministic — same input, same activation vector, zero features differing by more than 1e-6 across ten repeats on three states — so nothing downstream was being mis-measured by jitter.

With the mechanical causes eliminated, the decoupling had to be about what the features mean. **Test 3 is the one that resolved it.** For the top-50 features by overall activity, I computed mean activation over collision frames (μ_c) versus over ball-in-air frames (μ_a), and the difference Δ = μ_c − μ_a, labeling each frame's collision/air status from the full-resolution Atari frame (since, per Finding 1, the model's own 64×64 input can't resolve the ball). The Δ ranking is strongly structured and highly reproducible: Δ correlates at Spearman +0.977 across two independent episodes, with top-10 overlap of 7/10. Features are emphatically *not* uniformly decoupled from events. Of the top-50, about 15 are collision-correlated (Δ > 0.1), 22 are flat (|Δ| ≤ 0.1), and 13 are anti-correlated (Δ < −0.1). Clean collision detectors exist and are well-separated:

| feature | μ_c | μ_a | Δ | character |
|--------:|----:|----:|----:|------|
| #1199 | 1.40 | 0.00 | **+1.40** | fires *only* at collision (μ_a is exactly 0) |
| #120  | 1.23 | 0.05 | +1.19 | collision detector |
| #1773 | 1.13 | 0.00 | +1.13 | collision-correlated by group mean (see below) |
| #27   | 1.04 | 0.04 | +1.01 | collision detector |
| …     |      |      |      | (22 features flat) |
| #316  | 0.03 | 0.58 | −0.55 | fires in air |
| #1364 | 0.82 | 5.57 | **−4.75** | strong air-flight / ball-tracker |

And there's the answer, in the last row. The feature at the *top of the magnitude ranking* — #1364, the single most-active feature in the SAE, the one the discovery panel shows first by default — is the most strongly *anti*-collision feature in the entire set. Its mean activation is 5.57 in mid-air versus 0.82 at the paddle. It tracks the ball through its flight and goes quiet at the moment of contact. So a user doing exactly what I did — watching the top-firing feature during a collision — sees it stay flat, because its job is to track the ball in the air, not to detect the hit. That's not a bug and it's not perception bias. It's a ranking mismatch. The collision detectors are real, robust, and reproducible; they just rank *below* the persistent ball-tracker by magnitude, because a feature that fires throughout the ball's flight accumulates far more total activity than one that fires for the handful of frames of contact. Magnitude ranks by duration × strength of firing, and a brief event loses that race to a persistent state every time.

A fifth test made the mechanism visible and, in doing so, caught me almost making an error. Test 5 traced five features over a 1200-frame episode and computed a per-event "lift" — mean activation in a ±1-frame window around each collision, divided by the feature's episode-mean activation. The genuine detectors stood out as discrete spikes locked to the collision markers: #1199 at 5.8× and 6.4× lift across two episodes, #120 at 3.7× and 4.5×. The air-tracker #1364 showed no collision lift (0.95×, 1.0×), confirming it's not a collision feature, and the anti-collision #316 was actively suppressed at collisions (0.17× in both episodes). But #1773 — which had looked like a clean collision detector in Test 3, with Δ +1.13 — showed *no* reliable per-event lift at all (1.0×, then 0.3×). Its trace explained why: it fires in sustained multi-frame blocks, a game-phase signal rather than an event spike, and the 20 collision frames Test 3 happened to sample fell inside one of those blocks, inflating its group mean. "Fires *at* the event" and "is active during a window that *contains* the event" are different claims, and a group-mean contrast can't tell them apart. The thing that caught it was the discipline of running two episodes: #1199, #120, and #316 held across both; #1773 didn't, and the disagreement was the signal. Two methods that mostly agree but disagree on one feature is the healthy outcome — the disagreement is where the false positive was hiding.

Here's the part that turns this from a critique into a usable recommendation. While building the alternative rankings, I'd implemented temporal stability — ranking features by how *consistently* they fire over a recent window rather than how hard. Of the five most temporally-stable features, four (#27, #1221, #1773, #1789) were in the collision-correlated top-10 from Test 3, versus roughly chance for magnitude. Stability surfaced the event-tied features that magnitude buried. That's evidence, not just principle, that a substrate-adapted ranking does better here — though I'll keep it honest: it's one game, one feature set, and the stability-vs-collision overlap is a single comparison. The Δ ranking it builds on is the reproducible part (+0.977 across episodes); the "stability beats magnitude" claim on top of it is suggestive.

The transferable version, stated plainly: activation magnitude is a poor importance signal on a world model, and not because the features lack meaning. They have meaning — they correlate with events strongly and reproducibly. Magnitude ranks by duration × strength of firing, so it surfaces persistent trackers over brief event detectors, and the feature a user expects for a momentary event sits below a feature that fires continuously. Before concluding "the SAE features don't match the events," check whether your *ranking* is even showing you event features. For discovering event detectors, event-conditioned contrast (a Δ score) or temporal stability is the right sort key; magnitude is the wrong one. Importance metrics have to be adapted to the substrate, and magnitude is a bad default the moment you leave LLMs.

## Finding 5: causal importance pipelines must use magnitude-independent injection

If magnitude is the wrong importance signal, the obvious better one is causal: rank a feature by how much *intervening* on it actually changes the model's behavior. This is the most defensible definition of importance — it measures the thing you care about directly — and I couldn't find published prior art for a causal importance ranking of SAE features in a world model, so it was the part I most wanted to ship. I built it as an offline pipeline: for each feature, run +scale and −scale intervention rollouts against a shared baseline (same seed, same frozen action sequence) and score by mean token divergence.

The first version produced an encouraging result. On a sample of the 24 most-active features, two seed states, ±5 intervention, across two independent runs, causal importance correlated with activation magnitude at Spearman +0.56 and +0.64. The single most-active feature (#1364) was also the most causal in both runs. The reading at the time was "magnitude is a weak-to-moderate proxy for causal effect — it predicts the very top but not the ordering below it," which is a clean, plausible, publishable-sounding finding. The top-5 causal overlap across runs was 4/5; features responded asymmetrically to amplification versus suppression by about 0.8 tokens on average. It looked like it worked.

Then integrating the causal toggle triggered a verification pass — the matched-dimension skepticism habit, applied to a ranking instead of a scalar — and it unwound the whole thing in three stages.

**Stage one: the magnitude confound.** I ran a population-level cross-check I should have run first: correlate the causal score against activation across features. It came back +0.37, and among the active features the causal score was nearly proportional to activation. The cause was in the injection. I'd written it magnitude-relative — `scale × activation × direction` — so each feature was perturbed in proportion to how active it already was. That's the *correct* semantics for the live intervention tool, where you steer a feature relative to itself. It's exactly wrong for an importance ranking, because a bigger perturbation causes a bigger downstream change, so the score becomes a re-expression of activation. #1364, with activation 5.9, was being injected at roughly magnitude 30 while most features got about 5. Its "causal rank 1" was an artifact of being hit six times harder. The encouraging +0.56/+0.64 correlation with magnitude wasn't a finding about causality tracking magnitude; it was the pipeline measuring magnitude twice. The trap is that this is invisible in the per-feature math — every feature's score looks like a clean causal measurement — and only shows at the population level. That's the lesson, and it's the most directly transferable thing in this post: a causal-importance pipeline must use magnitude-*independent* injection, or it silently collapses into a magnitude ranking, and the only way you'll catch it is to correlate the score against activation across all features before you trust it.

The fix is one line. Inject a fixed norm for every feature regardless of its activation:

```python
unit = sae.W_dec[fid] * norm_std          # raw-space feature direction (W_dec is unit-norm)
# fixed-norm: ref = 1.0 for ALL features → injection = scale * unit_direction,
# identical magnitude regardless of activation.
ref = 1.0 if args.fixed_norm else max(float(seeds_feats[s][fid]), MAG_FLOOR)
direction = (scale * ref * unit).detach()
```

**Stage two: the fix exposed a deeper problem.** Fixed-norm injection removed the confound — the correlation with activation dropped to roughly zero (measured between −0.1 and +0.2 across runs, versus +0.37 confounded). But the scores collapsed into a narrow, noise-like band, and a reproducibility check came back *bit-identical* across re-runs, including across different torch seeds. That's the kind of result that should make you suspicious rather than happy, and the cause was instructive: the Atari environment reset and the trained, near-argmax policy are *effectively deterministic*. Every run — and every re-seed — warmed up to the *same* seed state. The causal score wasn't noisy; it was reflecting essentially *one game state*, perfectly. The magnitude confound had been masking this, because activation-scaled injection varied enough between features to look like a spread. Re-seeding can't fix a deterministic pipeline; the diversity has to come from sampling different *points* along a playthrough, which meant time-sampling states rather than re-running from the same reset.

**Stage three: with genuine state diversity, the signal is real but not robust enough to rank.** Averaging over diverse states widened the band and surfaced real structure. With the confound gone, #1364 is causal rank 1 *consistently* — which is now an interesting result rather than an artifact: it confirms the air-tracker genuinely drives the imagined dynamics even though it corresponds to no visible on-screen event, a real prediction/model-internal feature. The collision detector #120 rises to the top as well. But the decisive test for a *ranking* is cross-set robustness: split the seed states into two disjoint sets, score both, and see whether the rankings agree. They agree only slowly, with sharp diminishing returns:

| seed states | cross-set Spearman | top-10 overlap |
|------------:|-------------------:|---------------:|
| 1 (single)  | ~0.0               | —              |
| 5           | +0.33              | 3/10           |
| 18          | **+0.49**          | 3/10           |

Going from 5 to 18 states — 3.6× the compute — bought +0.16 of Spearman. Reaching a bar I'd trust to rank ~2K features (something like Spearman ≥ 0.6) would take many tens of states, and as the next finding explains, that compute is more expensive than it looks.

So I made a decision against a rule I'd committed to before seeing the number: integrate causal importance as a discovery ranking *if and only if* cross-set Spearman ≥ 0.6. The measured +0.49 is below 0.6, so causal importance did not ship as a discovery-panel ranking. The pre-committed threshold is what made this a clean go/no-go instead of an argument with myself about whether 0.49 felt good enough — it didn't matter how it felt. The discovery panel keeps two axes, magnitude (kept explicitly as the churny LLM baseline, for contrast) and temporal stability. Causal importance survives as a *characterization* tool: the script plus the per-feature endpoint reliably flag the top handful of causally-potent features (#1364, #120, #1773) for individual analysis, which is genuinely useful, but it isn't reproducible enough to rank the whole dictionary as a primary discovery surface.

The net of the arc: of three proposed importance axes, two survived (magnitude, stability) and one was demoted to characterization after the confound and the single-state limitation were found and measured. That's the honest outcome, and I think finding *why* causal didn't make it — magnitude coupling, deterministic seed states, and the cost ceiling below — is a more transferable result than a third ranking would have been.

The transferable version: causal-importance pipelines need explicit confound-checking against magnitude (correlate the score against activation, always), and a causal/intervention-effect ranking on a world model is single-state-fragile — a deterministic environment plus a confident policy means "add more seeds" diversifies nothing unless the states are actually different. Verify state diversity with two disjoint state-sets, and treat a single-state causal score as a per-state probe, not a feature ranking.

## Finding 6: autoregressive rollouts are dispatch-bound, not GPU-bound

The reason "just use more states" wasn't an option deserves its own finding, because it's a practical ceiling on this entire class of work and it surprised me.

The causal pipeline rolls the world model forward one token at a time in a Python loop — autoregressive generation with a KV cache, which is inherently serial: token N+1 depends on token N. Each individual forward pass is tiny, the kind of thing a GPU finishes in microseconds and then sits idle waiting for Python to dispatch the next one. At the scale a robust causal ranking needs — 18 states × 80 features × 2 signs × 10 steps × ~17 passes per step works out to roughly 490k serial forward calls per run — the wall-clock cost is almost entirely per-call dispatch overhead, not compute. I confirmed it the blunt way: CPU time ≈ wall time, CPU pinned near 100%, GPU near-idle.

The consequence is that the usual lever doesn't work. Moving from an M1 to a rented A100 barely changed the per-call rate, because the bottleneck was never the math. Running two such jobs in parallel made it *worse* — they contended for CPU rather than parallelizing, because it's a CPU-dispatch-bound workload, not a GPU one. I spent real time and a little money learning this, including leaving a cloud GPU idle and billing while the actual work ran locally because the GPU wasn't helping. The real fix — batching the rollouts so all features, states, and signs go through as a few thousand batched forwards instead of a million serial ones — was identified but not implemented; it's the thing I'd do first if I picked causal importance back up.

The transferable version: budget *time*, not just GPU dollars, for autoregressive interpretability work, and check where the bottleneck actually is before you rent hardware. A warm A100 does nothing for a pipeline that's waiting on Python dispatch between microsecond forwards. Throwing a bigger or second GPU at a serial autoregressive eval loop is wasted money; batching is the only thing that moves it.

## What was built

The findings above are what I'd want a reader to leave with, but they came out of a working tool, and the tool is worth a short concrete tour since it's the substrate that made the findings visible.

The main surface is a canvas of pinned feature cards, a pattern borrowed from LLM SAE tools where features have stable identity instead of existing only for the current frame. Each card is a feature: a draggable rectangle on the canvas with a sparkline of its recent firing history, an intervention-scale slider, and an editable label. The layout is backed by a server-side store, so a working set of features survives reloads — the thing v1 most lacked, where a feature in the live list existed for one frame and was then gone.

Discovery is search-first. You can jump to a feature by ID or by label, which is how you build up a working set deliberately rather than chasing a churning list. The top-firing list is still there, demoted to a side discovery panel with a metric toggle, where it now sits next to temporal stability as a second axis — the two rankings that survived. Clicking a feature there pins it to the canvas.

Intervention is multi-feature. Each pinned card carries its own scale, and the rollout endpoint sums the directions of all non-zero-scale cards into a single vector added to the residual stream, so steering several features at once costs the same as steering one. You pause the model at a state, set scales on whatever cards you want, and run an N-step imagined rollout (20 steps, 2 seeds by default) that produces side-by-side baseline-versus-intervened frames and the token-divergence trajectory beneath them — the load-bearing measurement from Finding 1.

The older general-purpose views — attention heatmaps, the attention-as-force-graph, residual-stream norm bars — were valuable while I was first poking at the model but didn't earn their screen space in the intervention workflow, so they're demoted to a hidden "Model Internals" tab. There's also an autointerp pipeline (top-activating frames per feature → vision LLM → cached label) that's built but not run by default; until it runs, most features show "unlabeled."

Under all of it is the unchanged IRIS infrastructure: forward hooks on each transformer block's residual stream (`backend/hooks.py`), the trained SAE (`backend/sae.py`), and IRIS's own imagination path for the rollouts. The redesign was a tool rewrite, not a model change.

## Methodological reflections

The single sentence that ties the six findings together is the one from the top: every LLM-SAE convention I tested transferred worse than expected, in ways that were individually subtle and collectively substantial. Pixel-space readout fails because the tokenizer is lossy below a resolution that reaches into the model's input. Rollout experiments measure against a near-static baseline because single-frame priming has no velocity. Intervention response is jagged because the decoder argmaxes. Magnitude ranking surfaces persistent trackers over event detectors. Causal ranking collapses into magnitude ranking unless you inject magnitude-independently, and even fixed it's single-state-fragile. And the compute lever you'd reach for to fix the fragility doesn't pull, because the pipeline is dispatch-bound. None of these is fatal; all of them need adapting before the convention is trustworthy.

The discipline that surfaced them is worth naming because it's reusable and it's most of what I'd attribute the findings to. Cross-check against the obvious alternative: the magnitude confound was caught only by correlating the causal score against activation across the whole population, a check the per-feature math gave no reason to run. Pre-commit the decision criterion: the Spearman ≥ 0.6 bar turned "0.49 feels borderline" into a clean no-ship, before I'd let myself rationalize the number. Document the negative result: causal importance not shipping, and the specific reasons it didn't, is the most transferable output of the project — more useful than a third ranking that worked would have been. And single-measurement skepticism throughout: two episodes is what demoted the block-confounded #1773; two disjoint state-sets is what exposed the single-state fragility; two runs is what showed the causal tail wasn't reproducible. Three times across this and the prior project, a single measurement misled a writeup, and the temptation to draw a conclusion from one run is strongest right after an honest reframe, when "negative" feels like the calibrated, mature answer. It isn't, on its own. This discipline carried over from the prior factored-SAE project ([{{FACTORED_SAE_LINK}}]), where it was also load-bearing — there, hard top-1 routing and dominant-chart-only evaluation were the matched-dimension corrections that kept soft-routing from leaking signal into the gating mechanism and inflating reconstruction quality. Same habit, different substrate.

## Limitations and what wasn't done

These are observations from one substrate, not laws, and the scope is narrow in ways worth stating explicitly so nobody over-reads them.

It's one game — Breakout — and one model — IRIS. The SAE is trained at a single layer (layer 5) of one model; whether features at other layers behave differently is open, and there's no cross-model replication at all. The SAE is small, 2048 features, and the harvest behind it (~80k vectors from ~5k frames) is on the low side for clean feature recovery — the usual rule of thumb wants more vectors per feature than this has, so some feature quality is left on the table. There's no comparison to alternative SAE architectures; this is one plain ReLU/L1 SAE, not a bake-off. The autointerp labels that would let a human read each ranking's top features by concept are built but not run, so "stability surfaces better features than magnitude" rests on the collision-correlation proxy rather than on read labels. And the causal-importance verdict is a *not-yet* as much as a *no* — it's below the robustness bar at the state counts I could afford, and batched rollouts might clear it; I haven't built them. Every "magnitude is a poor signal here" claim is a Breakout-layer-5 claim until someone replicates it elsewhere.

## Conclusion

There are two things to take from this. The first is a catalog: pixel-space readout, rollout baselines, intervention smoothness, magnitude ranking, causal-importance injection, and autoregressive compute are all places where an LLM-SAE convention or instinct needs adapting before it's imported into world-model interpretability, and for each one there's a specific failure mode and a specific adaptation. The headline is magnitude ranking — on this substrate it reliably shows you a persistent tracker (#1364, the air-flight feature) when you're looking for an event detector (#1199 and #120, the collision features that exist and are robust but rank lower), and that single mismatch accounts for a complaint that looks like "the features are broken" but isn't.

The second is the example of the discipline that surfaces such things: cross-check against the obvious alternative, pre-commit the decision criterion, document the negative result, and distrust any single measurement. That discipline is what turned "the features feel decoupled" into "magnitude ranks the wrong features, and here is exactly the one you were watching and why," and what turned "causal importance looks promising" into "it's a magnitude ranking in disguise, and here is the population-level check that proves it." The conventions are good conventions; they were just built for a different substrate, and finding out where they bend is most of the work.
